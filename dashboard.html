Investment Rules

Your personal reference guide — read this before investing in any stock or ETF

🧠 The Golden Rules
1
Understand before you invest. If you cannot explain in two sentences what the company does and how it makes money — do not buy it. Complexity hides risk.
2
Buy businesses, not tickers. You are buying a share of a real company. Think like a business owner, not a gambler. Would you want to own this entire business?
3
Price is what you pay, value is what you get. A great company at a terrible price is a bad investment. A mediocre company at a great price can be a good one. Always compare price to value.
4
Time in the market beats timing the market. Nobody can predict short-term moves consistently. Invest regularly, hold patiently, let compounding do the work.
5
Margin of safety. Only buy when the price is meaningfully below your estimate of fair value. This protects you when your analysis is wrong — and it will be, sometimes.
6
Never invest money you cannot afford to lose. Markets can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent. Keep 6 months of living expenses in cash, separate from investments.
📋 Before Buying Any Stock — Checklist
✅ Must Pass
  • Profitable — positive EPS and free cash flow
  • Revenue growing year-over-year
  • Debt/Equity below 2 (or below industry average)
  • P/E reasonable vs industry peers
  • I understand the business model
  • Management has a good track record
  • Competitive advantage (brand, patents, network effect)
⚠️ Questions to Answer
  • Why is it cheap if it looks cheap?
  • What is the bear case — what could go wrong?
  • Who are the competitors? Is the moat shrinking?
  • What % of my portfolio will this be? (<10% per stock)
  • What is my exit plan if the thesis breaks?
  • Am I buying because of fundamentals or hype?
  • Would I be happy holding this for 5+ years?
🟢 Positive Signals
  • P/E below sector average
  • ROE above 15% consistently
  • Profit margin improving over 3 years
  • Dividend growing every year (for income stocks)
  • Payout ratio below 60%
  • Insider buying (management buys their own shares)
  • Price near 52-week low, not high
🔴 Red Flags — Think Twice
  • Negative free cash flow for 2+ years
  • Debt/Equity above 3
  • Dividend yield above 8% (often unsustainable)
  • Payout ratio above 80%
  • Revenue shrinking for 2+ consecutive quarters
  • CEO or CFO resigned recently without explanation
  • Stock up 200%+ in the last 12 months on no news
  • Everyone around you is talking about it (buy the rumour...)

📦 Before Buying Any ETF — Checklist
✅ Always Check
  • TER (expense ratio) — below 0.20% for major indexes, below 0.50% for niche
  • AUM — at least $500M (too small = risk of closure)
  • Index tracked — know exactly what you are buying (S&P 500 ≠ MSCI World)
  • Replication — physical preferred over synthetic (swaps)
  • Dividend policy — accumulating (auto-reinvests) vs distributing (pays out)
  • Currency — hedged vs unhedged (hedging costs ~0.5-1%/year)
💡 Good ETF Rules
  • Broad market ETFs (MSCI World, S&P 500) beat most active funds over 10+ years
  • One global ETF + one bond ETF = simple, effective portfolio
  • Accumulating ETFs are more tax-efficient in most countries (including Switzerland)
  • Rebalance once per year — do not touch in between
  • Never pay more than 0.50% TER for a broad index fund
  • Sector ETFs (tech, healthcare) = higher risk, use max 20% of portfolio
⚖️ Portfolio Construction Rules
Diversify but not too much. 10–20 stocks across different sectors is enough. More than 30 and you are just buying a very expensive index fund.
Maximum 10% in any single stock. Even your highest conviction idea can be wrong. Position limits protect you from catastrophic single-stock risk.
Geographic diversification matters. Do not put everything in Switzerland or the US. MSCI World gives you ~70% US automatically — consider adding emerging markets.
Age-based allocation. A rough guide: % in bonds = your age. At 52, consider ~50% equities / 30% bonds / 20% alternatives. Adjust for your own risk tolerance.
Rebalance annually. If one position grows to 20%+ of your portfolio, trim it back. Do not let winners turn into concentration risk.
Keep cash ready (5–10%). Market crashes create buying opportunities. If you are fully invested, you cannot act when prices drop 30%.
🧘 Behavioural Rules — The Hardest Part
Never sell in panic. Every market crash in history has eventually recovered. Selling during a crash locks in permanent losses and usually happens at exactly the wrong time.
Never buy on hype. By the time everyone is talking about a stock, the easy money has already been made. "Hot tips" from friends, news, or social media are late by definition.
Do not check prices every day. Daily price checking leads to emotional decisions. Review your portfolio monthly, or quarterly. Checking more often = worse decisions.
Separate the stock from the story. You can admire Apple products and still find the stock overvalued. Investing in companies just because you like them is an emotional bias.
Be greedy when others are fearful. The best buying opportunities come during market crashes, recessions, or sector crises — when everyone else is running away.
Keep an investment journal. Write down why you bought each position. When you want to sell, re-read it. Has the original thesis changed — or are you just reacting to noise?
🇨🇭 Switzerland-Specific Notes
  • Stamp duty (Stempelsteuer): 0.075% on Swiss securities, 0.15% on foreign — charged on every buy and sell. Factor this into your calculations for short-term trades.
  • Withholding tax on dividends: Switzerland withholds 35% on Swiss dividends. You can reclaim most of it via your tax return (DA-1 form).
  • US dividends: 15% withholding tax for Swiss residents (covered by US-CH tax treaty). Reclaimable via DA-1.
  • Capital gains: Tax-free in Switzerland for private investors — this is a major advantage. Do not waste it on short-term trading (you become a "professional trader" if too active).
  • Wealth tax: Your portfolio is part of your taxable wealth (Vermögenssteuer). Declare every year at year-end market value.
  • Pillar 3a: Max CHF 7,258/year (2024) — fully tax-deductible. Use this for long-term equity exposure first before taxable accounts.
  • DEGIRO costs: Low fees but charges connectivity fee (€2.50/month per exchange). Stick to major exchanges to avoid extra costs.
